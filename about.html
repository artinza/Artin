The Story
Born in Tehran.
Raised everywhere.
Built by movement.

I was born in Tehran, Iran, to parents from Khoy — an Azerbaijani city in the northwest. At 8, we moved to the Netherlands, where I spent five years. Then Toronto. Three countries, multiple languages, and more airports than most people see in a lifetime.

That kind of life doesn't let you get comfortable. It teaches you to adapt fast, solve fast, and move forward — to not dwell, not over-attach, and to find your footing in any environment. Every time I landed somewhere new, I had to rebuild: friendships, language, identity. You learn to treat disruption as data, not disaster.

There was never a single eureka moment that turned me into an engineer. Engineering is a lifestyle — it seeped in through my father, a software developer who always had something interesting to show me. We share knowledge like a feedback loop: I learn something new, I go explain it to him. That back-and-forth sharpened me more than any classroom.

At 17, I'm in my senior year at Earl Haig. I've chosen to pause, breathe, and grow as a person — my social confidence, my charisma, my sense of self. I've built things that impressed engineers twice my age. Now I'm building myself. That's the hardest project yet.

01 — Method
Zoom In,
Then Zoom Out

I break every problem into its smallest components — understanding each part's role, properties, and purpose. Then I step back and see how they interact. The full picture only makes sense once you've understood every piece. That's how you find the gaps others miss.

02 — Mindset
The Limit Is
The Starting Line

I'm not interested in what's already been done. The interesting work happens at the edge of what's considered possible. I treat limitations as specifications — they define the shape of the solution, not the reason to stop.

03 — Inner Game
Quiet the
Inner Critic

From tennis I learned this: the self-critical voice doesn't make you better, it makes you slower. Real performance comes from presence — trusting your training, silencing the doubt, and letting your natural ability take over. On the court and in the lab.

04 — Engineering

A Koenigsegg One:1. A Saturn V. An F-22. These are not machines — they are the frozen decisions of brilliant people who refused to compromise. Engineering done right is indistinguishable from art. I hold my own work to that standard.

05 — Life
Adapt.
Don't Dwell.

Moving between countries taught me that attachment to a fixed reality is a liability. Problems — external or internal — are best solved fast and completely. Learn what they're teaching you, then move forward. Stagnation is the only real failure.

Operating System

Mani
festo

These are not aspirations. They are the principles I have already tested — on the court, at the workbench, in new countries, and in my own head. This is how I actually operate.

AX · 01
Engineering Is a Lifestyle, Not a Career

I don't switch engineering on when I sit at a desk and off when I leave. I look at a vent and think about airflow. I watch a plane and calculate lift. I hear a noise in an engine and wonder what's failing. This is not a job description — it is a way of perceiving everything. You either live it or you don't.

Philosophy
AX · 02
Understand Every Component Before You Judge the System

The most dangerous thing in engineering — and in life — is forming an opinion about the whole before you understand the parts. I force myself to zoom in first: what is this piece? What is its role? What are its constraints? What does it interact with? Only then do I zoom out. Skipping this step is how catastrophic failures are built.

Method
AX · 03
The Limit Is Information, Not a Wall

When I hit a boundary — technical, personal, social — I treat it as a data point. It tells me about the shape of the problem, the properties of the system, the gaps I haven't filled yet. Limits are not reasons to stop. They are the most useful thing a problem can tell you.

Mindset
AX · 04
Silence the Critic. Trust the Training.

Tennis taught me this clearly. The moment you start narrating your own failures mid-performance, you've already lost. The inner critic doesn't improve your output — it interrupts it. Real precision comes from presence, not self-surveillance. Learn from the miss, then let it go and play the next point.

Inner Game
AX · 05
Disruption Is Data. Don't Dwell.

I've moved countries multiple times, rebuilt social circles from zero, learned new languages mid-childhood. Each time, I had one choice: adapt or stagnate. I learned to treat upheaval as a fast-track education. Disruption strips away what isn't essential and shows you what you're actually made of. The faster you process it, the faster you grow.

Resilience
AX · 06
Precision Is a Form of Respect

When I build something — a CAD model, a dashboard, a piece of code — the level of precision I bring to it is a statement about how seriously I take the work. Sloppy tolerances, lazy naming conventions, poorly documented code: these are not small things. They are a signal of how much you care. I care. The detail shows.

Craft
AX · 07
Build Something Worth Lasting

I believe I'm on this earth to contribute to something that outlives me. Whether that's a breakthrough in aerospace, a machine that changes how people live, a company that redefines a field — I don't know the exact form yet. But the direction is clear: go further than what came before, and do it with enough craft that it's still worth something long after you're gone.

Purpose
AX · 08
The Most Important Project Is You

I've built dashboards, vents, trading systems, wings. None of them required the effort of building a version of myself I'm proud of. Engineering skills compound. So does confidence, charisma, self-awareness, and emotional intelligence. At 17, I know what I'm capable of technically. Now I'm going deeper — building the person behind the engineer.
